### Route Options — Snow Line Decision

This is the crux of the trip planning. Two routes to Mt Daniels' summit, and the better choice depends on where the snow line sits.

#### Route 1: Scatter Creek → Peggy's Pond → Lynch Glacier
*Best when snow line is above ~4,000'*

| Segment | Miles | Elev Range | Notes |
|---------|-------|------------|-------|
| Scatter Creek TH to trail junction | 2.5 | 3,400' → 4,200' | Steeper approach through forest, gains elevation fast |
| Trail junction to Peggy's Pond | 2.5 | 4,200' → 5,600' | Open meadows, nice skinning above ~4,200' |
| Peggy's Pond to Summit (Lynch Glacier) | 2.0 | 5,600' → 7,960' | Up the glacier, steepening near top |
| **Total one-way** | **~7 mi** | **4,560' gain** | |

**Why choose this when snow is high:** The Scatter Creek trail climbs steeply out of the gate, reaching skinnable snow (~4,000–4,200') within the first 2 miles. You spend less time carrying skis on dry trail. Once at Peggy's Pond, you're in a stunning alpine basin with a direct shot up the Lynch Glacier — an east-facing moderate-angle glacier that's the classic ski descent on Daniels. E-aspect = earliest corn, so you can summit and ski by 10–11 AM.

**Ski descent:** Lynch Glacier, E-facing, ~2,400' of continuous 25–35° skiing from summit to Peggy's Pond. The premier ski line on Daniels. Sunday's corn cycle favors this aspect first.

#### Route 2: Tucquala Meadows → Hyas Lake → Cathedral Pass → South Side
*Best when snow line is below ~3,800'*

| Segment | Miles | Elev Range | Notes |
|---------|-------|------------|-------|
| Tucquala Meadows TH to Hyas Lake | 4.0 | 3,450' → 3,450' | Flat valley-bottom trail, great skinning if snow-covered |
| Hyas Lake to Deep Lake | 2.0 | 3,450' → 4,400' | Gradual climb, forest |
| Deep Lake to Cathedral Pass | 2.0 | 4,400' → 5,600' | Steeper, alpine |
| Cathedral Pass to Summit (S slopes) | 2.0 | 5,600' → 7,960' | Traverse & climb S/SW glacier slopes |
| **Total one-way** | **~10 mi** | **4,510' gain** | |

**Why choose this when snow is low:** The first 4 miles along the Cle Elum River to Hyas Lake are nearly flat at 3,450'. If snow is continuous at that elevation, this is *incredibly* efficient skinning — flat track-setting through old-growth forest with virtually no dry-trail bootpacking. You trade 3 extra miles of distance for zero wasted effort carrying skis. The S/SW slopes above Cathedral Pass corn later (11 AM–12 PM) but offer a wider, more forgiving ski surface.

**Ski descent:** South-face glacier slopes, S/SW-facing, ~2,400' from summit to Cathedral Pass area. Wider and lower-angle than Lynch Glacier. Corn timing is later — plan to ski this between 11 AM and 1 PM.

### Gear Considerations

- **Ski crampons:** Essential. The morning crust will be bulletproof until 9+ AM. If you don't have ski crampons, bring boot crampons for the upper glacier.
- **Boot crampons + ice axe:** Carry for the summit block and any icy sections on Lynch Glacier.
- **Sun protection:** High priority. Clear sky + snow + late April = extreme UV. Glacier glasses, SPF 50, lip balm.
- **Light insulation:** Morning temps will be in the mid-20s. A puffy for summit/transitions is enough — you'll be skinning in a base layer by 8 AM.
- **Whippet or self-arrest capability:** Lynch Glacier steepens near the top. On firm morning snow, a fall here would be fast.
- **Avy gear:** Beacon/shovel/probe for glacier travel and any slope >30°. Even at LOW danger, wet loose can catch you in a terrain trap.
